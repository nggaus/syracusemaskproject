Update -- 11:00 AM, 4/7/20  
Calico Gals has added a new program called the Family Mask Project. Calico Gals is donating mask fabric kits to anyone making masks for family, friends and neighbors. Here's how the program works:

  • Each kit makes 4 masks. Request mask kits by emailing marcia@calicogals.com.
  • Due to the shortage of elastic, they recommend t-shirt fabric for ties. These are easy to make and comfortable to wear. Even kids can help. Click here for an excellent pattern and video for making ties and masks.
  • Please do not request mask kits if you are making masks to sell to others. We appreciate and respect your work, but this project is specifically for families in need.

Update -- 6:00 PM, 4/2/20   SUNY Upstate has made minor updates to their document "How to Make Facemasks". If you downloaded my alternate assembly instructions (same pattern/style) prior to today, please download it again as I have added even more tips to make assembly both easier and faster. Note that Upstate's instructions call for either elastic ear loops or four corner fabric ties, and my alternate instructions give you the option of making one long fabric tie (which saves fabric and is very comfortable). I don't know if the one tie is confusing -- see video below on how it works. I welcome feedback on that. Also, Upstate's instructions use a pipe cleaner or floral wire for a form-fitting nose piece. My instructions call for a metal strip, but the pipe cleaner or floral wire will work here too.

PLEASE -- If you are sick or have potentially been exposed to someone who is sick, please wait until you are recovered and out of danger before sewing masks!

Masks needed in central New York

As you have likely heard, there is a national shortage of surgical masks. Hospitals are in desperate need. They are beginning to take hand sewn masks as a stop gap until the shortages are alleviated.

There are plenty of patterns and advice online, and a lot of people jumping in to make masks, but it wasn’t clear to me that there was any organized effort for central New York (maybe, but haven’t been able to find any). I wasn’t willing to proceed until I knew that 1) what I was making was acceptable; 2) I knew where they were needed locally; and 3) I knew how to deliver them without creating more confusion at busy hospitals and medical facilities.

I finally got answers. SUNY Upstate and other hospitals, laboratories, healthcare centers, and agencies are taking donations from people who are willing and able to sew masks. A hospital committee at SUNY Upstate looked into the materials and design needed to make them as safe as possible for their healthcare workers to use. That design, and others, is posted on the DESIGNS page.

FULL DISCLOSURE:
The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention does not endorse fabric masks as a sole preventive measure to shield healthcare workers from the coronavirus. Due to the extreme shortage of PPE in the United States, however, hospitals are requesting hand sewn masks to wear over top of standard CDC-approved N95 masks in order to extend the life of the approved masks. Additionally, some healthcare faciities are running out of N95 masks and are using hand sewn fabric masks in liu of wearing no face protection at all. I have listed several hospitals on my "Drop Off Sites" page which are asking for hand sewn masks to extend or supplement their dwindling supplies. I have made every effort to include links to the instructions these hospitals have released for masks that they will accept. See the "Sewing Groups" section below for more information on locally requested mask styles.

If you are here looking for information how to make a personal mask for yourself or your family for everyday public use, you can try any of the designs here that are requested by the hospitals. If you want to keep it simple, the New York Times has an easy mask that you can make from household items. Another good mask tutorial recommended by Calico Gals is here.
